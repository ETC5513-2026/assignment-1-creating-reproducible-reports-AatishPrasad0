Introduction

This analysis aims to investigate the relationship between customer purchasing behaviour and targeted marketing expenditure. It seeks to determine whether increased marketing spending can have a significant influence on consumer behaviour. We also account for a variety of influential factors, such as how often the customer is transacting, how long the customer has been transacting, as well as the type of customer. This provides a more rounded, multivariate basis for our analysis. Understanding the the relationship between these factors and the importance of their interconnectivity is an important step in optimising business decisions.

Research Question section (2 points)

This report investigates whether an increase in marketing expenditure would have a significant increase in customer purchasing expenditure. It also considers how factors such as visit frequency and customer segment influence this relationship The purpose of this report is to identify the key drivers of purchasing behaviour and make a comparative analysis of marketing expenditure against other factors on this behaviour.

Data Set Introduction

The data set contains customer purchasing behaviour in relation to marketing expenditure along with other variables. These include customer_id, which uniquely identifies the customers, the customer segment(whether they are individuals, small businesses or enterprises) and notes about the interaction between the customer and the organisation recording these observations, all of which are stored as strings. There is also marketing_spend (amount spent on marketing to a customer), the number of visits, and the total amount the customer has spent on purchases, which are recorded as double attributes. Finally, we include a signup_date, which is a Day attribute recording the date when the customer first made a transaction.

To clean the data set, we ran the distinct function to remove duplicates, converted the “N/A” records into R-readable “NA” and converted the date column into a standard format for further operations.

Results section (5 points)

Figure 2 illustrates the purchase amount spent by customers relative to the amount of money spent marketing to them. We note that there seems to be a positive trend in this relationship, suggesting that the higher the marketing spend, the higher the money spent by customers on purchase transactions. This is supported by the upward sloping fitted line, once again suggesting a positive association between the two variables.

Figure 3 shows the relationship between the purchase amount spent by customers and the number of visits, across customer segment. We note that higher number of visits generally corresponds with higher purchase expenditure across all segments. Interestingly, the plot reveals that for customers with fewer visits, the Enterprise segment leads the pack in purchase amount, followed by a huge drop. The next highest spender for fewer visits are Small Businesses, with Individual Consumers taking the lead only when the number of visits exceed 7.
